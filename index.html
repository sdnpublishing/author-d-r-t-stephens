The Beginner's Guide to Sigil Magic

One of the most accessible forms of operative magic — from intention to activation.

Sigil magic is one of the most accessible forms of operative magic available to beginners. Unlike traditions requiring years of study, sigil work can be practised with nothing more than pen, paper, and a clear intention.

At its core, a sigil is a symbol charged with intent — the word derives from the Latin sigillum, meaning seal. The modern approach, popularised by Austin Osman Spare, involves condensing a written intention into an abstract symbol, charging it through focused will, then releasing it into the unconscious.

The process: write your intention as a clear, present-tense statement. Remove repeated letters and vowels. From the remaining consonants, construct an abstract glyph — combining, overlapping, transforming shapes until the original letters are unrecognisable. This is your sigil. Activate it through a moment of intense focus, then deliberately forget it. The work has been delegated to deeper processes.

Why Sacred Geometry Is More Than Mysticism

The mathematical patterns in ancient sacred sites and their relationship to modern physics.

Sacred geometry describes the mathematical patterns underlying both ancient architecture and the structures of modern physics. The Platonic solids appear not only in Greek philosophy but in nature itself: the icosahedron in viral protein structures, the hexagonal lattice in honeycomb and graphene.

Medieval cathedral builders encoded specific proportions not merely as decoration, but as participation in what they understood as the fundamental grammar of creation. When a medieval monk and a twenty-first century physicist independently arrive at the same set of forms through completely different routes, something is worth paying attention to.

The Beginner's Guide to Sigil Magic

One of the most accessible forms of operative magic — from intention to activation.

Sigil magic is one of the most accessible forms of operative magic available to beginners. Unlike traditions requiring years of study, sigil work can be practised with nothing more than pen, paper, and a clear intention.

At its core, a sigil is a symbol charged with intent — the word derives from the Latin sigillum, meaning seal. The modern approach, popularised by Austin Osman Spare, involves condensing a written intention into an abstract symbol, charging it through focused will, then releasing it into the unconscious.

The process: write your intention as a clear, present-tense statement. Remove repeated letters and vowels.
